§ 01 Findings

Three things the risk surface gets wrong.

01 HeatmapHigh

The heatmap is theatre.

A 5×5 red-yellow-green grid feels rigorous. It isn't. The axes are subjective, the cell colours encode nothing a sentence couldn't, and the aesthetic "dashboard red" has no place in a tool that wants to feel considered. A heatmap is how a committee reassures itself; a steward wants to read the risk out loud.

Remove
No heatmap. Replace with one sentence summarising the quarter: "Three questions open. One is moving."
02 FramingHigh

A risk is not a row, it's a question.

Risks are listed as short titles with severity chips — "FX exposure · High", "Key-person · Medium". A title and a chip reduce a living concern to a tag. Each risk should be framed as the open question it actually is, with a one-line note on what you're doing about it.

Rewrite
"Can the CFO step back in Q3 without the audit stalling?" followed by "Continuity plan drafted · successor not yet confirmed."
03 SeverityMedium

Five levels is three too many.

Risks are tagged H/M/L-H/L/Vanishing — five buckets that no one can tell apart in practice. The chip colour distracts from the content.

Reduce
Two states only: open (a dot in ink) and moving (a dot in amber). Everything else is a sentence.
