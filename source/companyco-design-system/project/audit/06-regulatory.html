The calendar.

Obligations are, structurally, a calendar — things that must be done by certain dates. The current view hides this under a checklist, so the reader can see what is done or due, but not when they'll be standing in the same spot a month from now. A calendar of commitments is what a steward actually holds in their head.

§ 01 Findings

Three things the checklist hides.

01 IAHigh

A checklist flattens time.

Obligations live on a timeline. Rendering them as a flat list of checkboxes destroys that shape. A reader can see "10 things due" but not "five this month, four next, one deep winter". The seasons of the business are invisible.

Group
Group by month. Eyebrow in mono, month name in serif italic. Items sorted by day within each month.
02 CopyMedium

"Complete" and "Pending" say nothing.

Each item is tagged Complete, Pending, or Overdue. These are states of a task manager. Obligations have their own language — filed, drafted, rehearsed, not yet due. Use it.

Rewrite
Filed / drafted / rehearsed / due / overdue — in italic serif at the end of each row.
03 UrgencyMedium

Red is a blunt instrument.

Overdue items turn red. Everything else is neutral. There's no visible sense of what's coming up — an item due in eight days reads the same as one due in three months. A weekend reader wants to feel the near edge of the calendar.

Add
A soft amber tint on items due within 10 days. Red stays reserved for overdue.
